# constellatio · latin conventions

Latin is used as a register marker, not as an erudition display. It carries
a structural distance the working language cannot — a way of saying *this
is the frame, not the claim*. The frame is the skill's contribution;
everything inside the frame should remain in the language the historian
was actually thinking in.

## Why Latin at all

The named layers exist *across* readings. The working language tilts toward
the reading it shares vocabulary with. The moment a layer is called "the
foundation reading" or "the basic position", it has already taken sides.

Latin sits above the dispute. It is dead enough to be neutral; it does not
align with any 二十世纪 reading of 五四, any 八十年代 reading of 王安石变法,
any current consensus about 鸦片战争. The chart's *grammar* needs that
neutrality. The chart's *content* does not.

So: Latin is the frame. The phenomenon, the stars, the era names, the
gravity sentence — all stay in the language they were thought in.

## Naming pattern

Pattern: `noun (+ classical descriptor)`, attested in classical or medieval
Latin. Two-word phrases preferred; one word is fine; three words is the
ceiling.

| Term | Use | Note |
|------|-----|------|
| Stellae fixae | Stars present in every reading | From celestial mechanics. The most stable layer name. |
| Constellationes temporum | Reading-by-era constellations | "Constellations of the eras". Each era draws its own. |
| Cartographia comparata | The overlay layer | "Compared cartography" — the chart of charts. |
| Polaris caveat | Warning against false synthesis | English noun + Latin noun, intentionally bilingual; the bilingualism mirrors the warning. |
| Gravitas | The gravitational core | Use *only* when the gravity is moral or political weight, not when it is a structural lacuna. The default term is "gravity" in plain English. |

Optional secondary terms (use sparingly, and only when the chart genuinely
benefits):

| Term | Use | Note |
|------|-----|------|
| Stellae mobiles | Moving stars (position varies by reading) | Acceptable; classical attestation thin but readable. |
| Stellae deficientes | Missing stars (omitted by all readings) | Borderline. Prefer plain English "missing stars" unless the visual rhyme with `Stellae fixae` carries weight. |
| Aporia | A reading-resistant remainder | Greek, not Latin, but classical and apt; retain when the report's honest conclusion is "cannot resolve". |

## What to avoid

- **Neologisms.** If no attested term exists, fall back to plain English.
  Inventing Latin to fill a slot is the exact failure mode this convention
  guards against.
- **Vocative or imperative grandeur.** `Audi!` `Ecce!` `O lectorem!` —
  never. The lens is austere, not theatrical. Latin is here to be cold,
  not loud.
- **Over-decorated cases.** `In medias res constellationis` is showing
  off, not naming a layer. If you cannot translate the term back into a
  one-line gloss, do not use it.
- **Latin-only output.** The audience is a Chinese-reading historian.
  Latin is a frame; never load meaning onto Latin alone. Every Latin
  layer name appears with its plain-language gloss the first time it is
  used in a document.
- **Pseudo-Latin science vocabulary.** `Constellatio quantica`,
  `Hermeneutica meta` — anything that smells like 19th-century journal
  taxonomy or 20th-century continental theory dressed in robes. The
  register is austere classical, not academic showcase.

## Test

> "If a Latinist would wince, rewrite it."

Apply at four points:

1. **Pronouncing the term.** If you cannot read it aloud without stumbling,
   it is too long or too constructed.
2. **Glossing the term.** If the one-line plain-language gloss is harder
   to write than the Latin, the Latin is doing the wrong work.
3. **Translating into the working language.** If the Chinese gloss feels
   tortured, the Latin term is not earning its place.
4. **Asking what the term forbids.** A layer name should make some
   readings *unsayable*. If the term lets every reading pass through, it
   is not actually a layer.

When in doubt, English-with-Latin-overlay
(`Stellae fixae · the stars every reading must include`) is preferable to
confident-sounding Latin that risks being wrong, vain, or both.

## On the title of the skill itself

`constellatio` (3rd-declension noun, *constellatio, -onis, f.*) is
attested in late Latin astronomy ("the standing-together of stars"). It
is the only Latin term used as a *standalone identifier* in this skill;
every other Latin phrase is paired with a gloss. The skill's name carries
the register; the chart's interior labels do not need to repeat it.
